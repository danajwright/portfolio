Six Months Into 2021, How’s That New Year’s Resolution Holding Up?

Yeah me neither.

Back in January, I downloaded the Calm app on the advice of a friend, thinking it would be nice to form a daily meditation practice.

Calm was cool. When I didn’t feel like meditating, I would listen to a short story. It was not meditating but that’s okay, I was rewarding myself for taking a little timeout, closing my eyes, and being still for 15 minutes.

Fast forward to May and I can’t remember the lat time I opened Calm or meditated.

Why didn’t it stick?

Did I get anything from trying it or was that a complete waste of time?

Daily actions compound

Perhaps not a complete waste, but also nothing compared to if I had stuck with it.

One of my favorite stories from the Calm app was Scottie Pippen’s history of basketball.

When he was in college, Pippen would get up early and shoot 200 jump shots every single morning.

He was a big man and knew that developing his outside shot would make him a much more dynamic player. He laments not getting a lot of shooting opportunities in regular practice, so he had to get it done on his own.

When Pippen speaks, his voice washed over me like warm butterscotch pudding. But I digress.

His jump shot ritual reinforces this idea that whatever I do repeatedly, I’m training to do that thing more in the future.

Slept in this morning? I trained my body to sleep in. Spent 90 percent of my free time today consuming content? I trained my mind to consume content.

Little habit machines

B.F. Skinner, one of the more controversial psychologists of the 20th century, claimed that emotions and thoughts actually have no power at all to influence our behavior.

That our choices and actions can be explained entirely with pattern analysis.

His research on mice and pigeons showed that animals respond to external stimuli in predictable ways depending on their environment. And the environment can be altered to achieve a desired outcome.

It’s the underlying psychology behind sin taxes. And the Calm app.

If you want to curb or eliminate a behavior, reinforce the costs associated with that behavior. If you want to inspire a new behavior, build a reward system to foster it.

Easy peasy, right?

Well, not exactly. Perhaps the reason no one knows how long it takes to form a habit is because it also depends on the unique values of the individual.

Let’s take two friends, for example, both of whom want to form a meditation practice:

Friend one grew up in a household where it was instilled in them at a young age that spirituality and mental health were the most important things in life. That their mind is a garden and needs to be nourished in various ways to grow strong.

Friend two had a much different upbringing. Where alcohol, junk food and trash tv were consumed regularly, and where there was no awareness of mental health or spiritual practice of any kind. But they read a Medium article about the benefits of meditation.

With the first friend, a deep cognitive and emotional association has taken hold. With the second, a simple, platitudinous monologue serves as motivation: “You should do this thing because it’s good for you.”

Now, they both download Calm, respond to the same triggers, and go through the same motions for a time.

Not to discount the power of a good medium article, but let’s be honest. Over the long haul, the first friend’s commitment and results will be orders of magnitude beyond the second.

The difference between success and failure isn’t just the environment or the pattern, it’s the mindset.

The paradox of new habits

We tend to see habits as leading indicators of success (like the Pippen example).

And yes, just doing something repeatedly until you eventually get good enough to enjoy it can work.

But with something sufficiently difficult, like, say, me learning transcendental meditation, that’s going to require intrinsic motivation. The kind of energy that comes from within — from your why.

Without a Scottie Pippen-level why, my mind reverts to its old ways, the routine becomes pointless torture, and failure is all but assured.

“If you take a lab mouse and measure its willingness to try new types of food and activities over the course of its life, and you map it to a human’s, it’s exactly the same curve.” — Robert Sapolsky

The takeaway

Our habits are not so much a leading indicator of success as they are a lagging indicator of our values.

Habits are built on a foundation of strong values, not the other way around. If the value is fragile, then the habit won’t be sustained.

We are not robots. We have agency. When we impose a new routine on ourselves without feeling the deeper reasons behind it, we deny the existence of that agency.

It’s one thing to say you like shooting jump shots, it’s quite another to wake up early in the morning, every morning, knowing it in your bones.
