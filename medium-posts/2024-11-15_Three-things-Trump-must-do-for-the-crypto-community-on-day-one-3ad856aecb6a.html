Three things Trump must do for the crypto community on day one

Or he’s dead to us.

“Never sell your bitcoin,” Trump told a cheering crowd at the Bitcoin Conference in Nashville, Tennessee in late July.

It was one of many overtures he made to the crypto community during the campaign coupled with a bevy of significant promises, like firing SEC Chairman Gary Gensler, banning CBDCs, creating a national Strategic Bitcoin Reserve, and ensuring that all Bitcoins are “made in the USA,” just to name a few.

And we ate it right up. The industry threw big money to Trump, contributing about 120 million dollars to his campaign. Investors also plowed billions of fresh capital into the asset class through the recently approved Bitcoin and Ethereum ETFs.

While it’s difficult to say exactly how highly Trump’s embrace of crypto factored in the minds of voters, poll after poll suggest that it was in fact a significant issue, and that this election saw the emergence of a pro-crypto voter block which broke largely for Trump.

While campaign promises should always be taken with a grain of salt (perhaps never more so than with this president), I think it’s safe to say that Trump will be more favorable to the industry than Biden.

Here are the three things I think Trump has to do on day one to signal to the community that he is in fact the crypto president.

1. Free Ross Ulbricht

This one is absolutely non-negotiable. If Trump doesn’t free Ross on day one, the message to the crypto community will be crystal clear: We got played.

If you don’t know the Ross Ulbricht story, read it here, or watch it here.

Ross’s project, the Silk Road was live from 2011 to 2013 and represented the first real use case for the bitcoin network. In addition to giving people a convenient way to buy drugs, it facilitated the distribution of Bitcoin to millions all over the world. According to the criminal complaint that sent Ross to prison, there were approximately 1,229,465 transactions completed on the Silk Road and total sales were equivalent to roughly $1.2 billion.

To make this happen, Ross built all kinds of innovative features to ensure a quality experience for buyers and sellers. There were escrow contracts that pegged the price of products to the US dollar, a comprehensive product review system, an auction mechanism to allocate seller accounts, etc.

It’s hard to understate how important this thing was to the early adoption and development of the bitcoin network.

You know the meme of the small dominos cascading into the big ones? If Satoshi launching the network in 2009 was the first tiny domino, the Silk Road would be like the second or third tiny domino. Without it, Bitcoin usage may never have spread beyond a handful of cypherpunks, let alone achieved the mass adoption it has today.

Fans of the Silk Road argued that the site made the drug trade much safer, fairer and more transparent. Meghan Ralston, a former harm reduction manager for the Drug Policy Alliance, said it was “a peaceable alternative to the often deadly violence so commonly associated with the global drug war, and street drug transactions, in particular.”

Yes, Ross committed crimes. He was convicted in 2015 for narcotics trafficking, hacking and money laundering. The crypto community accepts this. What we can’t accept is the way he was unfairly scapegoated by prosecutors and the absurdly punitive sentence he received, which was “double life” plus 40 years in prison.

The US government under Trump is now poised to embrace Bitcoin in a number of material ways, essentially leveraging a monetary network whose ascendency Ross is responsible for, while Ross himself languishes in prison.

There is no moral logic that can possibly square that.

Free Ross on day one.

2. Create the Strategic Bitcoin Reserve (SBR)

The US Marshal is currently sitting on about 200k Bitcoins (much of which was confiscated from the Silk Road back in 2013).

Trump’s plan to establish the SBR is to simply send the Bitcoins from the US Marshals Service to the Treasury Department. It sounds simple enough, but the very act of the US Marshal sending the Bitcoin to the Treasury Department would signal a tectonic shift in the way the US government views this asset.

The Marshals Service essentially manages the Bitcoin like like a fleet of exotic cars it confiscated from a drug lord. They might hold it for a period of time if the value is appreciating, but their mandate is to liquidate it. To that end, the Marshals Service regularly auctions off massive lots of confiscated Bitcoin.

The Treasury Department on the other hand maintains something called the Exchange Stabilization Fund (ESF), which was created by the Gold Reserve Act of 1934. The fund consists primarily of gold, US dollars, foreign currencies and other credit instruments. It is used to manage exchange rates and backstop currencies through foreign exchange intervention.

The fund’s net position at the end of 2023 was $39 billion. At Bitcoin’s current value of about $90k, adding 200k Bitcoin to the SBR would add $18 billion to the fund.

And that might be just the beginning. Senator Cynthia Lummis has a bill that goes much further, ordering the Treasury Department to acquire 1 million Bitcoins (about 5 percent of the total supply) to hold long term in the SBR.

The idea of the US government buying up strategic assets isn’t new. In the 19th century, the US purchased Manhattan, California, Alaska and the entire North American midwest (the Louisiana Purchase), all resulting in multi-trillion dollar returns for the county. The US makes lots of other strategic purchases all the time, stockpiling things like gold, oil and grain.

It’s a pretty simple idea: figure out where the value is going to be, go buy as much of it as you can for as cheaply as you can, and hold it.

El Salvador and the Kingdom of Bhutan have proven that Bitcoin works as a sovereign reserve asset, so the cat is out of the bag. The only rational thing the US can do now is to make sure that on the off chance the world starts moving to a different reserve asset other than US treasury bills, the US also owns a huge chunk of that asset.

3) Make small Bitcoin transactions tax exempt

There’s a bar in New York called PubKey which accepts Bitcoin for purchases. If I go there and buy a pint for $7, the current tax rules say I’m supposed to record that as a sale of a capital asset. I’m supposed to note the dollar amount I paid for that $7 worth of Bitcoin when I bought it (my basis) and the dollar amount the Bitcoin is worth when I buy the drink. At the end of the year, I owe capital gains tax on the difference.

Needless to say, the tax treatment on Bitcoin makes it incredibly difficult for people to spend in small amounts.

The Bitcoin whitepaper published by Satoshi in 2009 is titled, Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System

The US government making a tax exemption for deminimus transactions, let’s say under $100, would allow for that original vision to be realized and demonstrate America’s commitment to the growth and success of Bitcoin.

In sum

Each of these initiatives could be done with the stroke of a pen on day one of Trump’s presidency. Sure, Elizabeth Warren and her goons will file lawsuits and try to block them, but they won’t hold up in court.

Once the Executive Orders are signed for the SBR and the tax exemption, we need to pass legislation that codifies them into law, that way they can’t simply be overturned by another president’s Executive Order. This shouldn’t be too much of a problem with Republicans in control of both houses.

There are plenty of other crypto and crypto-adjacent initiatives that can and should be done as well, but this is the bare minimum we need to see on day one.

Otherwise, he jeeted us.
