How crypto lending is reinventing the idea of credit — for the better

Traditional finance works on unfair handshake agreements, opaque business relationships, and selective enforcement. CeFi could level the playing field.

It can be painful to watch traditional finance professionals try to reason about crypto, however Bloomberg columnist and former investment banker Matt Levine pretty consistently provides analysis worth considering.

In a recent column, he wrote that the fundamental difference between crypto and traditional finance comes down to the extension of credit.

The cypto way

In crypto, when credit is extended through an on-chain lending platform, be it for hedging risk or for levering up a trade, the rules are strict and harsh.

You either have the collateral on deposit to meet your margin requirements or you don’t. You agree to terms when you take out a loan against your crypto. The terms are applied by an algorithm and enforced by a liquidation mechanism that is completely automated.

This is necessary for it to be permissionless.

There is no longer a need for credit checks, accreditation, or any other barriers to entry that might require human involvement (and fallibility).

Pros

  • Permissionless: Capital markets are accessible to anyone with a crypto wallet and an internet connection
  • Fast: Credit can be extended instantly
  • Transparent: Everyone has access to the same information which levels the playing field

Cons

  • Liquidations happen more frequently
  • Borrowers need tools to hedge the increased risk, which don’t exist yet
  • Securities and real world commodities don’t yet have reliable on-chain synthetics, so for now all the innovation that happens in this area only pertains to crypto assets

The TradFi way

In TradFi, credit is extended contractually. The biggest borrowers consist of PE firms, market makers, corporations and hedge funds.

According to Levine, banks make loans to these entities “based on a thick set of information.”

Reputation and credit worthiness are basically merged into one. Business relationships come into play. How valuable is this customer overall? Do they represent a line on further business? Were we in the same fraternity? Those sorts of things.

Murky forces 🤝

And the same things come into play on the enforcement side. For example, an account executive at a bank notices that one of his clients is getting very close to a margin requirement. He calls the client. No answer. Oh shoot, it’s the Friday before a three day weekend.

The lender now has to decide if he wants to liquidate the client, which would be the safest thing to do, but also rude and could destroy the relationship. Or if he should circle back after the holiday, stomaching the increased risk in the meantime.

Sometimes the flexibility afforded by human intervention works to everyone’s benefit. And sometimes it doesn’t…

Pros

  • Fewer liquidations
  • Active management may avert unnecessary market dislocation in certain scenarios

Cons

  • Active management can result in liquidations that take out entire firms and cause much more extreme market dislocation
  • It’s opaque and full of information asymmetries that benefit a tiny group of large account holders
  • It’s heavily permissioned (special treatment is only available to the privileged few with massive accounts)

What happens next

It should come as no surprise that the vast majority of liquidations we saw in the recent meltdown took place on crypto platforms.

They were swift, automatic and there were no “hey, just wanted to give you a heads up” type phone calls.

Only pain.

Actually to be fair, some platforms did send emails. I myself received this one about 15 minutes a͟f͟t͟e͟r getting liquidated on Deribit.

But these kinds of instant liquidations only go one level deep (the small customer level).

Crypto whales with billions of dollars don’t use exchanges or retail lending platforms to borrow, they do OTC deals (over the counter).

And OTC deals are done the TradFi way — through intermediaries.

More liquidations 📉

Legal contracts, relationships, handshake deals.

This is why I suspect we have not seen all the liquidations yet.

I suspect there are quite a few whale accounts that are currently underwater, but that have managed to negotiate various kinds of deals with their lenders.

Maybe they’re securing additional funding. Maybe they’re unwinding other positions. Maybe they are saying they are doing these things and really just praying that the market bounces.

In any case, some will survive and some will not.

Therefore, more liquidations are probably coming. These ones will be carefully managed, either by the banks or by the courts. But they will likely be much bigger than the ones we’ve seen thus far.

Forest fires provide a helpful analogy for how risk builds up in a system: Account managers who protect their clients from small to mid-sized liquidations act like a bunch of firefighters putting out small brush fires all the time.

This insulates customers from the consequences of their bad decisions and allows leverage to build up in the system, much like the overgrown brush provides fuel for a forest fire to spread.

Inevitably though, a fire catches that burns so hot that it incinerates the entire forest, including all the old growth trees.

The SBF model

While far from perfect, the crypto way of handling margin calls and liquidations seems more equitable and healthier overall than the TradFi way.

Crypto billionaire and CEO of FTX, Sam Bankman Fried agrees.

Last month, he submitted a proposal to the Commodities and Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) that would allow crypto exchanges to eliminate the clearinghouse and its members from the equation all together.

If approved, the proposal would allow exchanges like FTX to serve as their own clearinghouses, settling trades on behalf of their customers.

The SBF model doesn’t make any distinction between large and small customers.

It doesn’t ask questions like, how big is this customer’s balance sheet, how good is this customer’s reputation, or how much business can we expect to do with this customer in the future…

To SBF, the customer is irrelevant to the credit decision. Only the account and the funds on deposit matter.

No more army of account managers making credit decisions based on uneven, incomplete information. Just code, pinging your account balance every 30 seconds or so.

How it evolves

There is no indication yet on the likelihood or timeframe for the passage of this proposal, although the CFTC Chairman did say, “This proposal could end up leading to more efficient trading execution, less risk in the system,” and that he is required to cultivate “responsible innovation.”

Whether it takes a year or five years, this seems basically inevitable.

In the near term, I think it will cause a lot more small to medium size liquidations. But as people get used to the unforgiving nature of the new system and new tools emerge that help manage the risk, there will be fewer and fewer of these.

On that front, here are two ideas I think will be very interesting to watch:

More inputs

Exchanges could take additional inputs which factor into a customer’s credit worthiness, and the terms of the loan.

Of course, any additional input would need to be trustless, on-chain and enforceable through the same liquidation mechanism. But maybe certain kinds of data could give the customer access to more leverage, or qualify them for a “grace period” to top up collateral in the event of a shortfall.

For example, the customer’s crypto wallet. If a customer’s wallet is worth 100x the collateral requirement for a trade, maybe they get a grace period to top up.

A v2 of this could be a more comprehensive “social score” based on the customer’s on-chain activities.

A team called Brahma.fi is working on this exact thing.

Theirs is called karma.

A new hedging tool

Another team is working on a crypto native way to hedge the increased liquidation risk.

Lyra.finance is a DeFi platform for crypto options trading. It has a set of public APIs that facilitate automated options trading. That means anyone can design a strategy, build a vault and leverage the options liquidity on Lyra.

Unlike perpetuals or futures contracts, options have a fixed funding rate, which makes them the perfect tool to precisely hedge against liquidation risk.

For more sophisticated traders and fund managers, a platform like Lyra is a no-brainer.

At some point the exchanges themselves will probably acquire/ integrate tools like this.

Why I’m bullish

To be clear, the point of the crypto model is not necessarily to be less risky than the TradFi model.

In a way, it just takes the risk of human intervention (mostly on the back end of the transaction) and moves it to the front of the transaction (where the customer weighs the variables and the odds of being able to meet the margin requirement for the duration of the loan).

It reflects a different philosophy. A less trusting, but more egalitarian philosophy.

In so many ways, crypto is an idea whose time has come.

This is just one more manifestation of that.
