The DAO of Wu

How a secret Wu-Tang Clan album sewed the philosophical seeds of the NFT movement.

“A lot of things in life are temporary, fleeting, impermanent. But remember this — just like our blockchain, Wu-Tang is forever.”

Jamie Johnson, PleasrDAO

This space truly never ceases to amaze me, and I believe the story of the secret Wu-Tang Clan album will be one that historians recall with a much deeper appreciation than it’s been getting in real time.

The concept of things being owned by a widely dispersed group of individuals who agree to follow a set of rules enshrined in computer code (a DAO) is still brand new. But a couple months ago, one of the more high profile ones did a $4 million deal with the US government.

I think it’s safe to say, these things aren’t going anywhere.

The Wu

Before we get too deep into the DAO side of things, first we must understand the Wu.

Back in 2015, the Wu-Tang Clan announced that they had secretly recorded an album featuring every artist in the group (except ODB, who passed away in 2004). The album took seven years to produce, contained 31 tracks and would be entitled, Once Upon a Time in Shaolin.

Instead of releasing the album like normal, they held an auction where only one single copy of the record was sold.

The concept of putting a single copy of something on auction was obviously not new. Its standard practice in the art world with rare paintings and sculptures. And it happens every day on eBay.

But this was the first time the “frame” of rare art had been applied to a freshly minted rap album. Records like this are typically released in a pretty formulaic way. They get a few spins on the radio for some hype, they get released on streaming services where the artist makes a tiny fraction of a percent on each stream, and the music pretty quickly finds its way to YouTube or other platforms where people listen for free and the artist makes nothing.

It has been my experience as an artist that the commercial value of creative output in our society is generally pretty low, except for in a precious few contexts where it is astronomically high.

Well, this album definitely intended to be in the latter category. For one, it came in a box that looked something like The Lost Ark of the Covenant. Inside that box was a silver-plated double CD case made by British-Moroccan artist Yahya, which itself was sheathed in leather.

It also came with tight legal restrictions on duplication, distribution and public exhibition. Most controversially, the rights to the album included a ban on commercial distribution until the year 2103 (88 years).

According to artist and producer, RZA the number eight was symbolic because there were eight original members of the Wu-Tang Clan, the numbers of the year 2015 add up to eight, and a rotated eight is the symbol for infinity, which was used in the album cover art for their second album, Wu-Tang Forever.

The reaction

In 2015, many interpreted the album auction the way the Wu-Tang Clan intended, as a principled act of protest. One of the most legendary hip-hop groups of all time giving the middle finger to the middleman.

While the Web 2.0 streaming era had supplanted the old recording industry, the basic structure remained in tact: corporate blood suckers siphoning off profits and leaving creatives hanging out to dry.

RZA and Cilvaringz explained that they were trying to transcend the conventions of a standard album. They felt the value of music was cheapened by streaming and online piracy, and with this album they hoped to return music to the value of fine art.

The distribution ban, according to RZA was necessary to maintain the integrity of the album as a work of art and to deflect notions of a publicity stunt.

That of course didn’t stop critics from calling it a stunt and claiming that cash still more-or-less rules everything around the Wu-Tang Clan.

The philosophy

It’s interesting to look back now at the two competing takes on the album auction: The principled stand vs. the scheme to get more money for the artists.

In 2021, we now know that those goals are not mutually exclusive. They are in fact perfect complements. The Wu-Tang Clan album auction and the crypto movement are rooted in the same philosophical idea:

You can be an activist and a capitalist at the same time.

Since the Wu-Tang Clan auction took place, an entire shadow economic system has been built around the realignment of economic incentives and the elimination of blood sucking middlemen.

In this new system, cryptography has replaced the need for banks. Automated market makers have replaced stockbrokers. Smart contracts are consuming legal and clerical services. And the NFT platforms launching on every major crypto network are changing the rules of ownership, licensing and royalties.

The roles of artist, collector, investor and programmer are quickly collapsing into one. You see it now with the generative art pieces, like Bored Apes or The Meebits. They have limited supply which ensures their rarity. Plus dedicated global communities of people who love the art, and who will continue building streams of value in the form of games, online experiences and spin-off art projects. Everyone on the same page. All working to create something greater that the sum of its parts.

It’s a powerful model. The more I understand it, the more convinced I become that that it could change the fate of our species.

The DAO

So the Wu-Tang album was basically v1 of an NFT, the only difference being that it used traditional legal contracts instead of smart contracts. But the parallels between what they did then and what’s happening now in crypto don’t stop there.

The Wu-Tang Clan itself provides a pretty good blueprint for a DAO.

It’s a collective of artists that came together to achieve something greater as a group, but at the same time respecting each member’s sovereignty, giving them the freedom to do their own thing if they want.

A so-called DAO, or decentralized autonomous organization is very similar, except its rules are encoded into a computer program.

A DAO can function on a distributed basis with no central authority or decision maker. No government, no police, no courts.

Corporations are organized under the laws of a specific jurisdiction and require human involvement in their governance. DAOs on the other hand operate under a pre-agreed upon set of governance rules maintained and executed on a blockchain.

While the law has long imposed the useful fiction of personhood on corporations, allowing them to sue or be sued under contracts and offer its members protections against liability, DAOs do not yet enjoy these privileges.

This certainly presents some challenges. Right now, it’s quite difficult for a DAO to participate in traditional commercial arrangements, hire service providers or resolve legal disputes.

But that doesn’t seem to be stopping people from creating them and joining them. I think governments will have no choice but to accommodate.

Vermont and Wyoming have already created schemes, permitting DAOs to register as blockchain-based limited liability companies. Wyoming provides additional liability protection for DAO members who organize as a limited liability company in the state.

PleasrDAO

The saga of disgraced big pharma CEO, Martin Shkreli (who won the Wu-Tang Clan album auction with a bid of $2 million) is 100% worth reading, but I won’t rehash it here.

[Spoilers ahead]

After the album was repossessed, the government ended up selling it at auction to cover Shkreli’s debts. It sold it for $4 million and the winner this time was a DAO.

That’s right, a DAO managed to do a deal with the US Marshall. Which, for all the reasons mentioned above was no easy feat.

But this was also not just any DAO. This was PleasrDAO, one of the more well known and well funded digital art collectives.

PleasrDAO started out as a loosely-affiliated group of super fans that pooled their funds together to win NFT auctions. Specifically, NFTs by a particular artist called pplpleasr, who makes next-level animations for DeFi protocols.

This eclectic group of art collectors, digital artists and entrepreneurs who first came together through a shared love of a particular artist has since gained a formidable reputation for acquiring culturally significant pieces, many of which transcend crypto.

Other works in their collection include the Edward Snowden NFT, which they bought for $5.4M.

Another is a generative art piece called Dreaming at Dusk, which they picked up for $2M. This one was created by a Itzel Yard (ixshells) in collaboration with the Tor Network. The NFT is derived from the private cryptographic key used to create Dusk, the very first onion service.

The proceeds of both of these sales went to fund non-profit groups dedicated to defending freedom of speech and online privacy, the Freedom of the Press Foundation and the Tor Project, respectively.

Final thoughts

The DAO model is allowing artists and programmers to derive value from their work in a way that just didn’t used to be possible.

I now look at traditional work arrangements, which essentially treat creatives as any other kind of production job and I see it as a vastly inferior model.

Decentralized Finance was the first phase of the crypto revolution, a battle I expect to rage on for a long time. But that doesn’t mean we can’t start merging some of the crypto concepts with the soul, like Larva Labs did. And the Wu-Tang Clan before them.

A new foundation has been laid by financial primitives. The next phase will be NFTs and DAOs.

This new alignment will empower creatives, help us grow into our strengths and reward us for pursuing our passions like never before.
