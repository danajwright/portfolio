The Kick is in The Discovery

An antidote to the goal-oriented life.

Richard Feynman was perhaps the greatest polymath of the 20th century, receiving a Ph.D., a Nobel Prize in quantum electrodynamics, and writing several best-selling books.

His life provides an interesting antidote to the goal-oriented model of ambition I’ve always followed.

For as long as I can remember, goals have served to orient almost all my pursuits. My pattern is to look ahead, predict what might make me happy in the future, and hone in on something specific.

But after reading one of Fenyman’s books, Surely You’re Joking, Mr. Feynman! I started to wonder:

What if that’s not the best way?

Feynman The Safecracker

One day in 1945, Feynman was bored at work (Los Alamos Lab where they birthed the nuclear bomb) and he thought of a prank to pull on his colleagues.

He knew they were a bit careless when dealing with the safes that stored top-secret documents, sometimes forgetting to lock them, or leaving them on factory settings. So Feynman let himself into their offices, opened their safes, and “borrowed” whatever documents he needed.

He made a little game of it, often leaving notes behind:

“I borrowed document no. LA4312 . — Feynman the safecracker.”

This soon caught the attention of the Colonel in charge of his unit, who put in place a new standard operating procedure: “If Mr. Feynman has been anywhere near your safe, change the code.”

Undeterred, Feynman developed new methods to crack the combos. The Colonel eventually upgraded the safes.

Feynman broke into those too.

His methods were advanced, and are still studied in the field of safe cracking to this day.

Feynman the artist

At age 45, having won the Nobel Prize, Feynman took an art class. He recalls being told by the instructor over and over again to “loosen up.”

Not really getting it, the instructor had him draw without looking at the paper. He kept his eyes on the model, not once looking down at the shapes forming on the page.

On his first try, his pencil broke at the very beginning and he had nothing but scratch marks on his paper. The second time, he was quite pleased with himself, remarking at the “funny, semi-Picasso like strength” in his work.

He writes, “I had thought that ‘loosen up’ meant ‘make sloppy drawings,’ but it really meant to relax and not worry about how the drawing is going to come out.”

Feynman the philosopher

“I think it is much more interesting to live not knowing than to have answers that might be wrong. If we will only allow that, as we progress, we remain unsure, we will leave opportunities for alternatives… In order to make progress, one must leave the door to the unknown ajar.”

“Study hard what interests you the most in the most undisciplined, irreverent and original manner possible.”

“We must leave room for doubt or there is no progress and no learning.”

“I’ve already got the prize…the prize is in the pleasure of finding the thing out, the kick is in the discovery, the observation that other people use it…those are the real things.”

The Takeaway

Goals are nice because they help to orient us. They give us a way to exert some control over an unknown future so that we can make a little progress without imploding from anxiety.

The problem arises when we mistake the orientation tool for the thing of value itself.

When our goals bind us to a singular outcome.

When we become too rigid, and miss out on the everyday things that can bring just as much joy as the outcome we’re so fixated on.

Feynman realized early on that he could make a pretty good drawing without looking at the paper.

He could keep moving, making his mind up as he moved, flowing from one thing to another, effectively making the goal and the process one and the same.

In the end, he achieved a great deal.

Perhaps we would all do well to rely more on our intuition, update our sense of what’s worth pursuing regularly, and don’t get trapped by a false sense of certainty.
